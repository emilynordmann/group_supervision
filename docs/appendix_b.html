A.2 Secondary data

If you’re using secondary data for your dissertation, this means that you’re not going to create data yourself, instead, you’re going to use data that already exists. Secondary data can offer several advantages such as saving time, resources, and providing access to a wider scope of data than might be feasible for you to collect yourself. Secondary data can come in several different forms.

Academic Research Data: Many researchers collect more data than they use in their studies. This unused data can sometimes be accessed and reanalysed to answer a different research question than was originally proposed. This data is typically rigorously collected and validated, making it a reliable source. Reanalysing this data allows you to expand upon the original research, or approach it from a new perspective.

Public Databases: Numerous governmental, institutional, or organisation-run databases exist that provide a wealth of demographic, health, societal, and other types of data. Such databases can be national or international in scope, offering a vast amount of data for potential research questions.

Social Media and Online Data: Internet sources such as social media platforms, forums, and blogs generate a vast amount of data every day. These sources can provide rich qualitative and quantitative data for various research questions. For example, a thematic analysis of posts on a specific topic on Reddit can offer insights into people’s attitudes, experiences, or behaviors. However, it’s crucial to ensure that any use of this type of data is respectful of privacy considerations and adheres to ethical guidelines.

It’s important that your research question is aligned with the data that is available to you. Start by formulating a broad research question based on the literature as you would for primary research and then start looking for sources of data that might allow you to answer that question. You’ll find that you probably have to go back-and-forth between your RQ and looking for data until you find a good alignment. This means the set-up part of your dissertation will take longer than primary data collection, but, you don’t then have the time-consuming process of data collection so it all balances out.

Finally, it’s essential to consider any ethical implications of your data sources and ensure you have the necessary permissions to access and use the data. Some secondary data sources may require you to obtain approval, provide acknowledgment, or adhere to specific usage policies. Always ensure to comply with these requirements and respect the rights of the original data collectors and participants.
